**YC:**

Dealing with the paradox that you don’t know what the costumer wants, and the costumer doesn’t know they want. Here is where the YC algorithm comes in (since they don’t know what they want until they’ve used it and see what is dumb, and you can’t know what they want until they tell you that):

YC-algorithm

1. Launch quickly MVP (this is the most important)
    
2. Get initial costumers
    
3. Talk to costumers and get feedback
    
4. Iterate (neq pivoting) – keep the costumer, keep the problem!

1st principles:

Example

1. Say batteries are expensive (problem/assumption)
    
2. Ask: is there something fundamental (as far down as the laws of physics) that makes the batteries expensive? We can add upp the material cost and the answer is no.
    
3. Thus, there is something in the manufacturing that makes it expensive.
    
4. Reiterate with focus on the manufacturing.
    

This process manages to get past the social layer of reality to actually find the truth in the physical/logical layer.

Essentially, it is like we work through mathematical proofs and physics problems. It is like thinking of the world as only the physics, assuming as little as possible.

**Other questions:**

Finance is a pessimistic industry. Tech is an optimistic industry. More specifically, finance is locally optimistic, globally pessimistic in the sense that we can make money, have growth on paper, but the world as a whole will remain static in its premises. In another sense, it is locally pessimistic, in that any 1 problem is unsolvable and unknowable, but globally optimistic in that the system is knowable.

The contrast is somewhat that tech is the “man in the arena“.
